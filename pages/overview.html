The story · two arcs

How the project unfolded

Foundation models like UNI2 turn whole-slide images into compact patch embeddings. The open question: how far do those embeddings transfer to clinically meaningful tasks on TCGA cohorts with only tens to hundreds of patients per cancer? The answer came in two arcs: first classification on a single cancer, then survival across eight.

Classification arc · TCGA-CHOL

How an overly easy task can mask a real signal, and how a properly balanced harder target reveals it.

Step 1 · Naïve setup

Tumor vs normal hit AUC ≈ 1.00: suspiciously perfect

Predicting tumor vs normal tissue with slide-level cross-validation gave a perfect score. Too perfect to be useful — and the same 15 participants had matched tumor + normal slides, a textbook leakage risk.

Step 2 · Leakage-safe CV

The task is saturated, not leaky

Switching to GroupKFold by participant (so the same patient never appears in both train and test) kept AUC at 1.00. The task is simply saturated on UNI2 features, not a leakage artifact.

Step 3 · A harder target

Tumor grade, balanced, N = 26 → AUC 0.80

Switching the target to tumor grade (G1/G2 low vs G3/G4 high) on a perfectly balanced 13:13 set of 26 slides, KNN-5 reached AUC = 0.80, a non-trivial, clinically meaningful distinction.

Step 4 · Statistical validation

The signal survives a permutation test (p = 0.005)

A 200-shuffle permutation test produced 0 / 200 null AUCs above baseline (p = 0.005, Phipson–Smyth corrected). The grade signal is real, not a small-N fluke.

Targets explored, and the two that were discarded

Two further targets were tested and documented as dead ends: BAP1 mutation (AUC 0.64, too class-imbalanced) and PBRM1 mutation (AUC 0.38, anti-correlated noise). The mutation cohort is simply too small (N = 36, 7–8 positives) for that signal to surface.

Best AUC by classification target. Tumor-vs-normal saturates; grade is the real, validated win; the two mutation targets fall at or below chance.

Survival arc · TCGA pan-cancer

Extending to a 1 608-patient cohort, and fixing the noisy cross-validation that was making small-cohort survival results uninterpretable.

Step 5 · The CV was noise

"RSF 0.71 ± 0.22" was hiding the absence of signal

5-fold survival CV on 55 CHOL patients reported RSF C-index = 0.71 ± 0.22, a 95% CI of roughly [0.28, 1.15], i.e. uninformative. Each fold had ~5 events, far too few to estimate a meaningful C-index. The wide std measured fold-split noise, not predictive power.

Step 6 · Pooled LOPO + bootstrap

The right tool for small cohorts

Switched to leave-one-participant-out: fit on N − 1, predict the held-out patient's risk, pool all N risks into one C-index, then bootstrap (event, time, risk) triples 1 000× for a 95% CI. Every event is used exactly once.

Step 7 · Scale to UNI2

1 608 patients across 8 cancers

Added the UNI2 cohort: 1 608 patients with valid survival (557 events, 1 051 censored) across COAD, STAD, LIHC, CESC, ESCA, READ, ACC and CHOL. LOPO runs per cancer, so a model trained on COAD never predicts CHOL.

Step 8 · Make C-index interpretable

"Does WSI add value beyond age and sex?"

Each output row now carries a clinical-only baseline (Cox on age + gender), a WSI + clinical combined model, a per-SD hazard ratio with 95% CI, and a median-split log-rank p-value, answering the marginal-value question in one table.

Where it landed. ACC and LIHC are clear wins (C-index 0.84 / 0.69); CESC, COAD and STAD show small-but-real signal; READ is a cautionary tale where WSI is actively anti-predictive; and the smallest cohorts honestly return "we can't tell." See the full results →
